## The Cosmos of the Shield

# The Cosmos of the Shield

First of all he formed a shield, both large and solid, adorning it all over with gold and silver.

and around it he threw a shining border, triple, glittering; and from it suspended a silver belt.

On the shield he wrought the earth, the heaven, and the sea, the unwearied sun, and the full moon;

on it also all the constellations with which the heaven is crowned: the Pleiades, the Hyades,

and the might of Orion, and the Bear, which they also call by the appellation of the Wain,

which there revolves, and watches Orion; but it alone is without a share of the baths of the ocean.

## City of Peace and Strife

# City of Peace and Strife

On the shield he also fashioned two beautiful cities of articulate-speaking men.

In one, marriages and feasts; they conducted brides from chambers through the city with brilliant torches,

and a bridal song was raised. The young men were dancing in a circle, and flutes and lyres gave forth sounds.

Women stood at their portals admiring, while a crowd gathered in the forum.

For a strife had arisen: two men strove for the ransom-money of a slain man.

The one affirmed he had paid all, the other denied; both sought judgment before a judge.

Heralds reined in the people; elders sat on polished stones in a sacred circle, holding staves.

They arose and adjudged in turn, while two talents of gold lay in the midst for the best judgment.

## City under Siege and the Ambush

# City under Siege and the Ambush

On the shield, around the other city sat two armies glittering in arms, plotting to destroy or divide its wealth.

The besieged secretly armed for an ambush; their wives and children and old men guarded the wall.

Mars and Pallas Minerva led them, both golden, clad in golden garments, like gods conspicuous.

They reached a river, a watering-place for cattle, and lay in ambush, wrapped in shining brass.

Two spies watched for sheep and curvated oxen; soon shepherds advanced, playing pipes, foreseeing no fraud.

The ambushers slaughtered the oxen and fair flocks of white-wooled sheep, and slew the shepherds.

The besiegers, hearing the tumult, rode up on swift horses and fought a battle on the river banks with brazen spears.

Among them mingled Strife and Tumult, and destructive Fate, dragging a dead man by the feet, her garment crimson with blood.

## The Ploughland and the Harvest Feast

# The Ploughland and the Harvest Feast

On the shield he placed a soft fallow field, rich glebe, wide, thrice-ploughed; many ploughmen drove their teams.

When they reached the field's end, a man gave them a cup of very sweet wine; they turned eager to reach the other end.

The field grew black behind, like ploughed land, though being golden-a great marvel on the shield.

He also placed a field of deep corn, where reapers reaped with sharp sickles in their hands.

Handfuls fell thick along the furrow; binders tied sheaves with straw ropes, three binders following.

Behind them, boys gathered handfuls and carried them, while the king stood silently over a furrow, rejoicing.

Apart, apostles under an oak prepared a feast, sacrificing one of the oxen; women strewed white barley as dinner for the reapers.

## The Vineyard and the Vintage Song

# The Vineyard and the Vintage Song

On the shield he placed a vineyard heavily laden with grapes, beautiful, golden; the clusters black, soon to be wine.

It was supported throughout by silver poles; around it a dark-blue trench, and a hedge of tin; a single path led to it.

Young virgins and youths carried the sweet fruit in woven baskets, bringing the vintage.

In the midst, a boy played a shrill lyre, and sang the beautiful Linus song with a slender voice;

and they, beating the ground in accompaniment, with singing and shouting, followed, hopping with their feet.

## The Herds of Oxen and the Lions

# The Herds of Oxen and the Lions

On the shield he fashioned a herd of lofty-horned oxen, made of gold and tin; lowing, they rushed to pasture beside a murmuring river.

Four golden herdsmen accompanied the oxen, and nine dogs, swift of foot, followed them.

But two terrible lions among the foremost oxen seized a bellowing bull; it roared loudly as they dragged it.

The lions tore the bull's hide, devouring entrails and black blood, while the dogs and young men ran up.

The shepherds vainly urged the fleet dogs, who refused to bite the lions, but stood near, barking.

Also he formed a large pasture of white sheep in a beautiful grove, with folds, covered sheds, and cottages.

## The Dance of Knossos

# The Dance of Knossos

On the shield the illustrious Vulcan embellished a dance, like that which Daedalus once composed for fair-haired Ariadne in wide Gnossus.

There youths and alluring virgins danced, hands on each other's wrists; maidens in fine linen robes, youths in well-woven tunics shining with oil.

They wore beautiful garlands; youths had golden swords suspended from silver belts.

At one time they moved lightly with skilled feet, as a potter tests a wheel; at another, they moved in series toward each other.

A large crowd stood around the enjoyable dance, delighted; and two tumblers began their song, spinning in the midst.
