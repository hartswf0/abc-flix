## The Shield of Heaven

# The Shield of Heaven

## Earth, Sea, and Stars

First of all he formed a shield, both large and solid, adorning it all over.

Around it he threw a shining border, triple, glittering, and from it a silver belt.

Five were the folds of the shield itself, but on it he formed many ornaments.

On it he wrought the earth, and the heaven, and the sea, the unwearied sun.

On it also, all the constellations with which the heaven is crowned.

The Pleiades, the Hyades, and the might of Orion.

The Bear, which they also call by the appellation of the Wain.

It there revolves, and watches Orion, but it alone is without a share of the baths.

The illustrious Vulcan wrought all these wonders with cunning skill.

He placed the vast strength of the river Oceanus around the extreme border.

The golden shield reflected the unwearied sun and the full moon above.

Silver and gold adorned the heavens wrought upon the broad surface.

The brazen rim and silver belt adorned the broad shield with cunning skill.

## The City of Marriages

# The City of Marriages

## Feasts and Festivals

On it also he fashioned two beautiful cities of articulate-speaking men.

In the one, indeed, were marriages and feasts throughout the streets.

They were conducting the brides from their chambers with brilliant torches.

Many a bridal song was raised by the joyful crowd gathered there.

The youths were dancing in a circle, and flutes and lyres gave forth sounds.

The women standing, each at her portals, admired the spectacle.

But the people were assembled in the forum, for there a strife had arisen.

Two men strove for the ransom-money of a slain man before the elders.

The people shouted to both, supporters on either side in the forum.

The heralds reined in the people with their staves of office.

The elders sat upon polished stones in a sacred circle.

They held in their hands the staves of the loud-shouting heralds.

They arose and adjudged in turn with solemn justice before all.

But there lay in the midst two talents of gold, to give to the best judge.

The shield showed a city of peace with golden light and the sound of lyres.

## The Siege and Ambush

# The Siege and Ambush

## Bronze and Blood

But around the other city sat two armies of people, glittering in arms.

A plan was agreeable to them, either to destroy it entirely.

But they [the besieged] did not yet obey, but were secretly arming for an ambush.

Their beloved wives and young children stood upon the wall guarding it.

Together with them the men whom old age possessed kept watch.

But they advanced, and Mars and Pallas Minerva led them, both golden.

On the shield they were clad in golden garments, large with their arms, like gods.

But the people were smaller when they reached a place fit to lay an ambush.

By a river, where was a watering-place for all cattle, there they settled.

They were wrapped in shining brass beside the murmuring water.

Then apart from the people sat two spies, watching for sheep and curvated oxen.

Two shepherds followed, amusing themselves with their pipes, foreseeing no fraud.

But they [the liers-in-wait] ran up and slaughtered the herds of oxen.

They slew the shepherds besides, and a great tumult arose among the cattle.

The besiegers, hearing the tumult, mounted their nimble-footed horses and pursued.

They formed a line and fought a battle on the banks of the river.

They wounded one another with their brazen spears in the fierce affray.

Among them mingled Strife and Tumult, and destructive Fate.

Fate held one freshly-wounded, and another unwounded, and dragged one dead.

The garment around her shoulders was crimsoned with the blood of men.

They were turned like living mortals, and fought, and drew away the bodies.

## The Fields of Plenty

# The Fields of Plenty

## Plough, Corn, and Vineyard

On it he also placed a soft fallow field, rich glebe, wide, thrice-ploughed.

Many ploughmen drove hither and thither, turning round their teams.

When returning, they reached the end of the field, a man gave them sweet wine.

They turned themselves in series, eager to reach the other end of the deep fallow.

But it grew black behind, and was like unto ploughed land, though being golden.

There he also placed a field of deep corn, where reapers were reaping.

The reapers had sharp sickles in their hands, cutting the stalks.

Some handfuls fell thick in series upon the ground along the furrow.

Others the binders of sheaves were tying with straw ropes behind them.

Three binders followed the reapers, and boys gathering handfuls supplied them.

Among them the king in silence stood at the staff, rejoicing in his heart.

Apostles apart, beneath an oak, were preparing a feast, having sacrificed a huge ox.

Women were strowing much white barley meal as a dinner for the reapers.

On it also he placed a vineyard heavily laden with grapes, beautiful, golden.

The clusters throughout were black, and it was supported by silver poles.

Around it he drew a dark-blue trench, and around a hedge of tin.

Young virgins and youths of tender minds carried the sweet fruit in woven baskets.

In the midst of them a boy played pleasantly on a shrill lyre.

He sung the beautiful Linus song with a slender voice.

They beat the ground in accompaniment, with singing and shouting, hopping.

## Herds and Predators

# Herds and Predators

## Gold, Lions, and Sheep

On it he also fashioned a herd of lofty-horned oxen made of gold and tin.

Lowing, they rushed from the stall to the pasture, beside a murmuring river.

Four golden herdsmen accompanied the oxen, and nine dogs swift of foot followed.

But two terrible lions among the foremost oxen seized a bellowing bull.

He, dragging, roared loudly, but the dogs and the young men ran up to him.

The lions, having torn the hide of the huge ox, devoured the entrails.

The shepherds vainly pressed upon them, urging on the fleet dogs.

The dogs refused to bite the lions, but stood very near, barking and flying them.

On it also the illustrious Vulcan formed a large pasture of white sheep.

The sheep were in a beautiful grove, with folds and covered sheds and cottages.

The silver belt of the shield shone above these pastoral scenes.

The golden garments of the gods seemed reflected in the herds of gold and tin.

The river ran murmuring beside the waving reed, past stall and pasture alike.

The oxen and cattle lowed as they rushed beside the water, a marvel of skill.

The brazen armor of the warriors gleamed not here, but peace reigned.

# The River Oceanus

## The Border of the World

But in it he placed the vast strength of the river Oceanus.

It ran around the extreme border of the well-formed shield.

The Bear alone is without a share of the baths of the ocean, watching Orion.

The constellations watch from above while the river encircles below.

The sea was wrought upon the shield, and the heaven, and the unwearied sun.
